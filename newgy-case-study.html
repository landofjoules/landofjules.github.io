Newgy Robo Pong Companion App

Meet Robo Pong

This is a Table Tennis Robot. Newgy developed these to allow professional and hobbyist table tennis players to practice Ping Pong when they don't have a partner. They are also used by many specialized coaches for drills and training.

For the 35 year life of the company, the product had remained quite similar, with only small material and mechanical improvements. Recently however, they developed a product with a head capable of positioning itself dynamically through software, allowing for precise ball placement anywhere on the table.

For their upcoming release, Newgy wanted to develop a mobile application that would allow players and trainers to utilize this new functionality. People would be able to tap a spot on their smartphone, and the robot would shoot the ball in that location. This would have to be done in a way that kept players and trainers focused on the game, and made sense to both a professional and enthusiast crowd.

The Design

Ease of use was critically important. The controls would need to be usable while the robot was pelting balls at the player. My large, bold controls with color feedback a worked really well, and they ended up speeding up general operation roughly threefold when compared to the button controller.

Ping Pong is a game where you have little time to think between moves. Success rests on developing excellent muscle memory for complex situations. For this reason, drills are a huge component of training; a good coach might have upwards of 40 different drills memorized, practicing movement, spin, form, and much more.

One of the most popular features of the latest model (the 2050) was the selection of drills available. However, with the limited display, creating custom drills was confusing for their user base, but this could change drastically with the possibilities of a mobile app.

I wanted trainers to be able to have a clear visual oversight of the entire drill, so they could design them based specifically around the player. The arrows on the balls indicate spin, anotherÂ critical skill in professional Table Tennis.

The Result

The app tested really well, both with trainers and with individual players. Users had little difficulty finding their way around the app, and learning how to use it. I worked with the embedded systems team to make sure the designs were implemented smoothly. The final product is expected to be released in 2018.
